The honest case

Why it works

  • Stowe Rec Path is a daily anchor — 5.3 mi paved, flat, along the river. Stroller + scooter friendly.
  • Swimming holes by early June — Moss Glen Falls, Sterling Falls Gorge, ranch camp spots
  • Restaurant scene punches above its weight — Hen of the Wood, Doc Ponds, Plate, Harrison's
  • Big ski houses sit empty in June = great deals on spacious rentals with yards
  • Ben & Jerry's factory 15 min away — the 4yo will remember this forever
  • Pre-tourist-season: locals are around, crowds aren't, everything's open but not slammed
  • Genuinely beautiful. Green mountains, wildflowers, covered bridges.
  • Adults can rotate brewery/dinner runs while kids sleep — town is compact

The honest downsides

  • No lake or ocean. River swimming and pools only. No beach days.
  • 4.5 hour drive with a 2yo. Not terrible but not nothing.
  • Small town. By day 5–6 you've eaten at most restaurants.
  • Nightlife is zero. After bedtime it's porch/firepit/board games.
  • Early June water is still cold. Swimmable but bracing.
  • Rain days with toddlers = limited indoor options beyond the house
  • Burlington airport is 45 min away
  • Cell service can be spotty depending on the rental

The honest case

Why it works

  • The coast is genuinely stunning. Penobscot Bay, harbor towns, lighthouses — it's the postcard.
  • Lobster pounds, clam shacks, the whole Maine food thing. Kids eat lobster mac & cheese, adults eat lobster.
  • Camden Hills State Park — Mt. Battie is a short hike (or drive) with jaw-dropping harbor views
  • Portland day trip (1.5 hrs south) is one of the best food cities in the US per capita
  • Tide pools, rocky beaches, harbor seals — genuinely magical for a 4yo
  • Early June = pre-summer crowds. You'll have the coast mostly to yourselves.
  • More "wow factor" than Vermont — your group will feel like they went somewhere special
  • Good rental houses with ocean views in Camden/Rockport area

The honest downsides

  • The ocean is COLD. Like 52°F cold. Nobody's swimming in the ocean in early June. Full stop.
  • 5.5 hour drive with a 2yo is a hard day. Probably need a real lunch stop midway.
  • Rocky beaches, not sandy. No building sandcastles, no easy wading. It's beautiful but not "beach vacation."
  • Portland airport is 1.5 hrs from Camden/Rockland. That's a real schlep on departure day.
  • Cooler and foggier than Vermont. Early June can be gray and 58°F for days at a stretch.
  • Less walkable than Stowe — you'll drive to most activities. No equivalent of the Rec Path.
  • Restaurants in midcoast towns are good but thin. Not Portland-level outside Portland.
  • Fog + toddlers + rocky coast = you're watching them like a hawk near the water

The honest case

Why it works

  • Sandy beaches. Real ones. The kids can dig, wade, splash. This is the only option with actual beach days.
  • Shortest drive of the three — 3.5 hrs including the Sagamore Bridge. Very doable with a 2yo.
  • Cape Cod National Seashore is legitimately stunning — miles of protected dunes and beach
  • Tons of kid infrastructure: mini golf, ice cream shops, playgrounds, bike trails, whale watches
  • The Cape Cod Rail Trail (22 mi paved) is a great Rec Path equivalent for strollers/bikes
  • Provincetown day trip is genuinely fun and unique — art galleries, whale watching, great food
  • Early June is pre-peak — beaches aren't packed yet, restaurants have tables
  • Huge rental market = lots of big houses with yards. Well-established vacation infrastructure.

The honest downsides

  • It's Cape Cod. It's not a "discovery." Everyone's been or knows someone who has. Zero novelty for most East Coasters.
  • Ocean water is still mid-60s in early June. Swimmable for brave adults but the 2yo won't last long.
  • The vibe is "family vacation" in the most generic sense. Mini golf and fried clams. Not charming-Vermont or dramatic-Maine.
  • Traffic on Route 6 can be miserable even in early June, especially weekends. One road in, one road out.
  • Restaurants are fine but not exciting. Lots of "seafood shack" energy, fewer destination-worthy spots.
  • Rental prices are already high in June — no off-season discount like Stowe ski houses.
  • The Cape sprawls. Lower Cape (Wellfleet/Truro) is beautiful but far from Upper Cape. You'll drive a lot between towns.
  • No real "town center" vibe unless you're in Chatham or Provincetown. It's suburban/spread out.

The honest verdict

Stowe is the easiest, most practical choice. Walkable, great daily rhythm with the Rec Path, excellent food, and off-season ski house rentals are a steal. If your priority is "minimize logistics, maximize chill," Stowe wins.

Maine is the most memorable choice. The coast in early June is genuinely special — long golden light, empty harbors, lobster on the rocks. Your group will talk about it more. But you pay for it: longer drive, colder, foggier, less walkable, and the airport is far.

Cape Cod is the most kid-friendly on paper — sandy beaches, ice cream, mini golf, whale watching. But it's also the least interesting for adults, the most expensive, the most generic, and Route 6 traffic will test your patience. It's a fine vacation. It's not a special one. Everyone at home will say "oh nice" instead of "tell me more."

Acadia is the dream destination — a national park on the ocean, carriage roads perfect for strollers, Cadillac Mountain at sunrise, tide pools everywhere. If this were adults-only, it might win outright. But the 6.5 hour drive with a 2yo is the longest by far, the weather is the coldest and foggiest, and flying home means either a tiny regional airport (BHB) or 3 hours to Portland. The park's reservation system also adds a planning layer you don't need on vacation.

With a 2yo and 4yo for a full week? Still Stowe. The daily walkability and contained environment matter more than you think when you're managing two little kids for seven days. Cape Cod wins on beach access but loses on everything that makes a week feel easy. Acadia and Midcoast Maine are trips you take when the youngest is 5+.
