Design Feedback & Direction

Thanks for the initial SketchUp designs — the overall structure and sliding mechanism approach is solid. I want to keep the two-panel sliding mechanism but push the design in a more refined direction. Here's my thinking, organized by topic.

1. Layout: Asymmetric, 4 Units + 2" Trim Border

My office isn't symmetrical — windows on the left, desk offset to the right — so I'd like the built-ins to reflect that. The room breaks into two zones:

  • Lounge zone (~44"): Eames chair, side table, near windows
  • Desk zone (~76"): Desk (55" wide), desk chair

The built-in wall matches these zones. Each zone gets a matched pair: one fixed deep section + one sliding panel of identical width. The panel split aligns with the zone boundary, and the desk-side pair is visible behind me on video calls — symmetry there matters.

The wall is 124" total. A 2" Studio Green trim border wraps the entire unit, leaving 120" for the built-in carcass: 22 + 22 + 38 + 38 = 120". The two panels total 60", which equals the bed cavity width exactly. Matched pairs ✓, trim reveal ✓, full-bed opening ✓.

Why 4 units, not 6: Earlier versions split the wider sections into two ~20" sub-columns each. But 20" vs 22" sub-column widths looked like a measuring error. With 4 single units, the width contrast (22" narrow / 38" wide) is dramatic and intentional — narrow columns for books, wide columns for display objects.

  • Desk pair: 36" shelves × 2

  • Panel B (38") slides over the desk bookcase (38") — perfect fit
  • Full bed opening clear (60") — bed folds down

Bed clearance when open:

  • Lounge side: 22" — enough to get in/out
  • Desk side: 38" — plenty of room, luggage goes here

2. Flush Design

I want the wall to read as one flat plane when closed — no depth steps between the sliding panels and the fixed sections.

Why flush matters: With proud panels, each zone would have one section at 14" depth and one at 22" depth. The depth step cross-cuts both zones, visually connecting them instead of differentiating them. Flush preserves zone integrity — each zone reads as one unbroken rectangle.

How it works: The fixed sections normally sit at the same 22" depth as the panels. Full 14" deep shelves (front of shelves at 22" from wall). When opening the bed, push the fixed sections back ~8" on a concealed mechanism, creating clearance for the panels to slide laterally across the front.

Mechanism: I'd like to explore a low-profile linear bearing on a concealed rail mounted slightly above floor level on the cabinet base. 8" of travel with end stops. Sealed from debris (not an open floor channel). This keeps the action smooth and the hardware hidden.

Question: Is this feasible? What kind of linear bearing/rail system would support the weight of a loaded bookcase section? What's the cost/complexity premium vs. standard proud panels?

3. Murphy Bed: Full Size (60" Cavity)

Per the carpenter's assessment, a queen mechanism won't fit — we'll use a full-size (double) Murphy bed with a 60" cavity opening. Mattress is 54" × 75".

Why this works for us: Guests are typically solo travelers (parents, friends crashing) or occasional couples comfortable sleeping closely for a couple nights. The 6" reduction from queen is negligible; the full is 16" wider per person than a twin. Smaller mattress also means the bed projects 5" less into the room when down, improving clearance.

Mechanism weight matters: The glass whiteboard on the bed panel face adds 50–80 lbs. Gas-strut mechanism should be spec'd for a full-size bed plus the whiteboard weight from the start.

4. Material Palette

Three-material approach:

  • Frame / structure + trim border: Farrow & Ball Studio Green (#474D4A), painted to match room walls
  • Shelves: Natural walnut — warm contrast against the green
  • Back panels: Darker stained wood or painted dark — creates depth behind objects
  • Hardware: Brass — exposed track, shelf rails on sliding panels, cabinet knobs, shelf pins

5. Adjustable Shelving

Instead of fixed shelf positions, I'd like brass shelf pins in finely machined holes across every unit. 32mm European spacing for the pin holes.

Why: Every unit has identical construction. The different shelf rhythms between the two zones (denser books in the lounge zone, taller display openings in the desk zone) become styling choices, not structural commitments. I can reconfigure anytime.

Question: For the 36" wide walnut desk-side shelves, what material and thickness will prevent sag under display weight? 3/4" solid hardwood, reinforced plywood with walnut edge, or something else?

6. Brass Details

Brass serves a specific role: it marks the parts that move.

  • Exposed brass track across the top of the unit — visible rail with rolling carriers. This is the hero detail of the whole piece. I want something that feels bespoke — machined brass, substantial rollers, library-quality.
  • Brass shelf rails on all units — thin brass rods with end posts running horizontally across each shelf. Rails are shelf-mounted, not frame-mounted: each walnut shelf ships as a unit with two small brass sockets mortised into the top surface (~1" in from each end), two ~2" brass end posts that drop into the sockets, and a brass rod threaded between the post tops. When a shelf moves on the 32mm pins, its rail moves with it — always the correct height, no frame hardware to coordinate with adjustable shelf positions. Posts lift out of their sockets if I ever need a rail-free shelf for an oversized book.
  • Non-slip shelf liner on all shelves — museum-grade, invisible against the walnut.
  • Brass shelf pins — tying into the material story
  • Brass knobs on all lower cabinets

7. Top Cap + Trim Border

No crown molding (there's none in the house). Instead:

  • Thick flat cap (1.5–2" solid piece) across the entire 120" built-in width
  • Recessed puck lights mounted in the underside of the cap, washing downward to illuminate the shelves
  • 2" Studio Green trim wraps the entire unit — top, bottom, both sides — giving the built-in a clean reveal from the walls, floor, and ceiling. Reads as deliberate architecture rather than furniture pushed against a wall.

8. Bottom Closed Storage

Closed storage across the bottom of all four units. Key constraint: no outward-swinging doors — my desk is directly in front of this wall.

  • Sliding cabinet doors on all four units — small panels that slide horizontally within the base cabinet, with routed finger pulls (no protruding hardware).
  • The lounge-side cabinet (22") will store spirit bottles and glassware inside.

9. Astragal

Where the two sliding panels meet: an astragal (vertical molding strip on the leading edge of one panel that slightly overlaps the other) to hide the gap. This seam aligns with the zone boundary and the center of the bed cavity.

10. Hidden Features (Guest Mode)

Glass whiteboard on the bed panel face

The inside face of the murphy bed door — mount a glass whiteboard here. When I slide the panels open without lowering the bed, I get a full-width 60" writing surface. When closed, completely hidden. The dual-reveal (whiteboard for work, bed for guests) is the concept.

Bedding storage

Pillows, sheets, and duvet stored in the lower cabinets when bed is folded up.

Pull-out nightstand trays

Slide-out walnut trays at mattress height on both sides of the bed opening. Phone, water, glasses. Tuck completely flush when not in use.

Built-in charging

Recessed USB-C outlets on both sides of the bed cavity at mattress height, near the nightstand trays.

Lighting

  • Puck lights in the cap provide ambient shelf lighting day-to-day and reading light when bed is open
  • Warm LED strip along the top of the bed cavity — eliminates the "head in a cave" feeling

11. Electrical

Plan for outlets/wiring:

  • Inside the bed cavity (LED strip + guest USB-C charging, both sides)
  • Inside the lower cabinets (for charging stored devices)
  • In the cap (for the recessed puck lights)
  • Behind the lounge bookcase (for future accent lighting)

Questions for Contractor

  1. Full-bed mechanism width: Please confirm exact cabinet width for the full-size mechanism. The design assumes a 60" opening — if your mechanism is slightly different, we'll adjust panel widths symmetrically to preserve matched pairs.
  2. Flush mechanism: Is a linear bearing rail system feasible for pushing the fixed sections back 8"? What's the cost/complexity premium vs. standard proud panels?
  3. Brass track: Best heavy-duty track system (finishable or claddable in brass) for a loaded 38" panel? This is the most visible piece of hardware — needs to feel bespoke.
  4. 36" shelf spans: Material and thickness for 36" unsupported walnut shelves that won't sag under display loads? Reinforced plywood with walnut edge, solid hardwood, something else?
  5. Panel B weight capacity: Max load on a 38" sliding panel with walnut shelves + brass rails + books/objects. Does the track support this?
  6. Glass whiteboard + full-bed gas struts: Can we mount a glass whiteboard (50–80 lbs) on the murphy bed panel face with a full-size mechanism? Gas struts need to be spec'd for both loads from the start.
  7. Electrical: Can we run electrical into the bed cavity, lower cabinets, and cap during the build?
  8. Puck lights in cap: Right fixture and spacing for recessed puck lights in a 1.5–2" thick cap piece?
  9. Shelf pins: Any concerns with 32mm European spacing brass pins at these shelf depths?
  10. Shelf-mounted brass rails: We want the rails to travel with the shelves (socket-and-post system mortised into each shelf, rod threaded between posts) rather than frame-mounted. What source do you prefer for the brass sockets, posts, and rods? Do you have a machinist you work with for bespoke small-hardware, or is there a library-quality off-the-shelf system that's known-good?
  11. Linear bearing for push-back: Product/system recommendation for the fixed section push-back mechanism?
  12. 2" trim border: How do you prefer to handle the painted trim reveal between the carcass and the surrounding walls/floor/ceiling? Mitered, butted, scribed?
