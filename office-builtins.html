Design Feedback & Direction

Thanks for the initial SketchUp designs — the overall structure and sliding mechanism approach is solid. I want to keep the two-panel sliding mechanism but push the design in a more refined direction. Here's my thinking, organized by topic.

1. Layout: Asymmetric, 4 Columns

My office isn't symmetrical — windows on the left, desk offset to the right — so I'd like the built-ins to reflect that. The room breaks into two zones:

  • Lounge zone (left, ~44"): Eames chair, side table, near windows
  • Desk zone (right, ~80"): Desk (55" wide), desk chair

The built-in wall matches these zones. Each zone gets a matched pair: one fixed deep section + one sliding panel of similar width. The panel split aligns with the zone boundary.

SectionWidthDepthTypeZone

Panel A~22"8" (shallow, sliding)Slides over lounge bookcaseLounge

(The wall is 124" total, so these widths fit exactly. The two panels need to total the bed cabinet width (~64") — confirm exact mechanism width and adjust panel widths accordingly. The fixed sections absorb any difference.)

Why 4 columns, not 6: Earlier versions split the 40" sections into two ~20" columns each. But 20" vs. 22" column widths looked like a measuring error. With 4 columns, the width difference (40" vs. 22") is dramatic and intentional — wide columns for display, narrow columns for books.

How it works:

  • Panel A (22") slides over the lounge bookcase (22") — perfect fit
  • Panel B (40") slides over the desk bookcase (40") — perfect fit
  • Full bed opening clear (~64") — bed folds down

Bed clearance when open:

  • Lounge side (left): ~22" — enough to get in/out
  • Desk side (right): ~40" — plenty of room, luggage goes here

2. Flush Design

I want the wall to read as one flat plane when closed — no depth steps between the sliding panels and the fixed sections.

Why flush matters: With proud panels, each zone would have one section at 14" depth and one at 22" depth. The depth step cross-cuts both zones, visually connecting them instead of differentiating them. Flush preserves zone integrity — each zone reads as one unbroken rectangle.

How it works: The fixed sections normally sit at the same 22" depth as the panels. Full 14" deep shelves (front of shelves at 22" from wall). When opening the bed, push the fixed sections back ~8" on a concealed mechanism, creating clearance for the panels to slide laterally across the front.

Mechanism: I'd like to explore a low-profile linear bearing on a concealed rail mounted slightly above floor level on the cabinet base. 8" of travel with end stops. Sealed from debris (not an open floor channel). This keeps the action smooth and the hardware hidden.

Question: Is this feasible? What kind of linear bearing/rail system would support the weight of a loaded bookcase section? What's the cost/complexity premium vs. standard proud panels?

3. Material Palette

Three-material approach:

  • Frame / structure: Farrow & Ball Studio Green (#474D4A), painted to match room walls
  • Shelves: Natural walnut — warm contrast against the green
  • Back panels: Darker stained wood or painted dark — creates depth behind objects
  • Hardware: Brass — exposed track, shelf rails on sliding panels, cabinet knobs, shelf pins

4. Adjustable Shelving

Instead of fixed shelf positions, I'd like brass shelf pins in finely machined holes across every column. 32mm European spacing for the pin holes.

Why: This means every column has identical construction. The different shelf rhythms between the two zones (denser books in the lounge zone, taller display openings in the desk zone) become styling choices, not structural commitments. I can reconfigure anytime.

Key concern for 40" wide shelves: These spans need serious material — 3/4" hardwood or reinforced plywood with a walnut front edge strip to prevent sag. Please confirm the right material and thickness for a 40" unsupported span.

5. Brass Details

Brass serves a specific role: it marks the parts that move.

  • Exposed brass track across the top of the unit — visible rail with rolling carriers. This is the hero detail of the whole piece. I want something that feels bespoke — machined brass, substantial rollers, library-quality.
  • Brass shelf rails on all columns — thin brass rods with end posts running horizontally across each shelf opening.
  • Non-slip shelf liner on all shelves — museum-grade, invisible against the walnut.
  • Brass shelf pins — tying into the material story
  • Brass knobs on all lower cabinets

6. Top Cap

No crown molding (there's none in the house). Instead:

  • Thick flat cap (1.5–2" solid piece) across the entire width
  • Recessed puck lights mounted in the underside of the cap, washing downward to illuminate the shelves.

7. Bottom Closed Storage

Closed storage across the bottom of all four sections. Key constraint: no outward-swinging doors — my desk is directly in front of this wall.

  • Sliding cabinet doors on all four sections — small panels that slide horizontally within the base cabinet, with routed finger pulls (no protruding hardware).
  • The lounge-side cabinet (22") will store spirit bottles and glassware inside.

8. Astragal

Where the two sliding panels meet: an astragal (vertical molding strip on the leading edge of one panel that slightly overlaps the other) to hide the gap. This seam aligns with the zone boundary between lounge and desk areas.

9. Hidden Features (Guest Mode)

Glass whiteboard on the bed panel face

The inside face of the murphy bed door — mount a glass whiteboard here. When I slide the panels open without lowering the bed, I get a full-width writing surface. When closed, completely hidden. This is the feature I'm most excited about — the dual-reveal (whiteboard for work, bed for guests) is the concept.

Bedding storage

Pillows, sheets, and duvet stored in the lower cabinets when bed is folded up.

Pull-out nightstand trays

Slide-out walnut trays at mattress height on both sides of the bed opening. Phone, water, glasses. Tuck completely flush when not in use.

Built-in charging

Recessed USB-C outlets on both sides of the bed cavity at mattress height, near the nightstand trays.

Lighting

  • Puck lights in the cap provide ambient shelf lighting day-to-day and reading light when bed is open
  • Warm LED strip along the top of the bed cavity — eliminates the "head in a cave" feeling

10. Electrical

Plan for outlets/wiring:

  • Inside the bed cavity (LED strip + guest USB-C charging, both sides)
  • Inside the lower cabinets (for charging stored devices)
  • In the cap (for the recessed puck lights)
  • Behind the lounge bookcase (for future accent lighting)
